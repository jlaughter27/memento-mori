## 2. What Makes a Children's Math Product Legit: The Credibility Checklist

The following eight dimensions separate products that get adopted in schools and trusted by parents from apps that feel like toys.

### 2.1 Pedagogy & Efficacy

- **Explicit instructional model**: The product names and explains its approach (mastery learning, CRA, spaced repetition, adaptive scaffolding). Top products publish their pedagogy on their website.
- **Published efficacy evidence**: The gold standard is a peer-reviewed RCT or quasi-experimental study with an effect size reported. The minimum bar is a third-party review (Evidence for ESSA rating, What Works Clearinghouse snapshot, or Common Sense Education learning rating). Self-reported improvement statistics ("34% test score increase") carry almost no weight with districts.
- **CRA progression**: Concrete manipulatives or digital equivalents → representational images/diagrams → abstract symbols. Products that skip straight to abstract algorithms lose struggling learners.
- **Mastery before progression**: Content gates exist. A student cannot skip to Grade 5 fractions without demonstrating Grade 4 prerequisite fluency.
- **Spaced review / distributed practice**: Skills that were mastered weeks ago are automatically revisited, not left untouched. Khan Academy's Mastery Challenge and Leitner-style promotion system is the archetype. Without spaced review, mastery decays within days.
- **Mixed review / interleaved practice**: Problems from multiple previously learned skills appear together, not blocked by topic. This is one of the strongest memory-consolidation strategies known from learning science research.

### 2.2 Standards Alignment

- **Explicit Common Core (or state) tagging on every skill**: Each lesson or problem set is labeled with the specific standard code (e.g., "3.OA.A.1"). Teachers and curriculum coordinators expect this.
- **Scope and sequence document**: A downloadable PDF or web page showing what skill is covered in which order, mapped to grade level. IXL, Zearn, and Khan Academy all publish these publicly.
- **Grade-level placement**: The app clearly communicates which grade's content a given skill belongs to.
- **State standard variants**: Prodigy and IXL offer TEKS, SOL, and other state-specific mappings in addition to CCSS, which matters for school procurement.

### 2.3 Content Depth & Structure

- **Diagnostic placement test**: An entry assessment that determines where the child actually is, rather than locking them to their enrolled grade. IXL's Real-Time Diagnostic requires 40–50 items and produces a grade-level score for each domain. Zearn places students using a grade-level diagnostic and scope-and-sequence map.
- **Mastery checks**: A defined threshold (e.g., IXL SmartScore 80, Khan Academy "Mastered" status) before a skill is considered complete.
- **Worked-solution / explain-it mode**: Students can see a step-by-step breakdown of any problem. This is both a learning scaffold and a trust signal to parents who want to understand how the app teaches.
- **Word problems**: Problems in context (not just naked computation) are a CCSS requirement and appear in all leading products.
- **Problem variety**: Multiple representations of the same concept (visual, numeric, word-problem, application). Single-format drill apps feel shallow.

### 2.4 Trust & Safety

- **COPPA compliance statement**: Explicitly stated in the privacy policy, with a description of what data is and is not collected from children under 13. Since the FTC's January 2025 COPPA rule amendments (full compliance deadline April 22, 2026), opt-in consent is the default and biometric data is explicitly protected.
- **FERPA alignment** (for school use): Third-party vendor agreement language, no secondary use of student data, deletion-on-request.
- **Third-party certification**: iKeepSafe COPPA Safe Harbor certification and/or Common Sense Privacy Seal (rated on a 0–100 scale) are the two credentials schools look for first. Prodigy's 92% Common Sense Privacy score is a competitive advantage.
- **No ads**: All leading products are ad-free for children. Ads in a children's learning app are an automatic disqualifier for school use and a major parent red flag.
- **Accessibility (WCAG 2.2 AA)**: Color contrast, text resize, alt text, keyboard navigation, screen reader compatibility. The ADA Title II update (compliance deadline April 2027 for state education agencies) is accelerating district requirements.
- **Dyslexia/reading support**: Font toggle (OpenDyslexic or Lexend), read-aloud for problem text. Approximately 10–15% of students have dyslexic processing profiles.
- **Screen-time controls**: Session length limits, parent-set daily caps, sleep-mode scheduling.

### 2.5 Parent & Teacher Features

- **Parent/teacher dashboard**: Skill-level progress, accuracy, time-on-task, mastery status per topic. Weekly summary emails.
- **Printable resources**: Worksheets, practice sheets, or score reports that can leave the screen. SplashLearn offers printable worksheets; Prodigy offers offline worksheet pairing. This signals the product respects non-digital learning time.
- **Assignment/goal-setting tools**: Teachers can assign specific skills or set targets. This is table stakes for any product targeting school use.
- **Progress reports by standard**: Reports organized by CCSS code, not just by lesson name. This is what curriculum coordinators and principals actually want to see.

### 2.6 Engagement Model

- **Intrinsic + extrinsic balance**: The strongest products (DreamBox, Zearn, Khan Academy) lean primarily on competence and progress as motivation. Products that rely heavily on external rewards (coins, loot, pet feeding) risk the over-justification effect and are viewed with skepticism by educators.
- **Growth mindset language**: Explicit "not yet" framing, effort praise, mistakes-as-learning signals throughout the UX.
- **No dark patterns**: No guilt mechanics, no fake urgency, no content locked behind upsells in ways that penalize children. The FTC is actively enforcing against dark patterns in children's apps.
- **Session arc with natural stopping point**: Not an infinite scroll. 10–20 minute sessions with a clear "you're done" moment.

### 2.7 Business & Credibility Signals

- **School adoption at scale**: Prodigy claims 800,000+ teachers annually; Zearn is used in 35% of U.S. elementary schools. District-level purchase decisions require this kind of social proof.
- **Awards from reputable bodies**: Common Sense Education Editor's Choice, Apple App Store Editor's Choice, Parents' Choice Gold, Children's Technology Review Editor's Choice, EdTech Digest Awards.
- **Evidence for ESSA rating**: The highest-weight independent efficacy signal for U.S. school procurement. Zearn, DreamBox, and IXL all hold Tier 1 ("Strong") ratings.
- **Research partnerships**: University partnerships and IES-funded studies (MIND Research Institute/ST Math, Carnegie Learning/MATHia) signal long-term investment in evidence.
- **Published pedagogy and scope/sequence**: A product with a detailed, publicly accessible curriculum framework reads as serious and academically grounded.

## 3. Gap Analysis for MathQuest: Offline Single-Device PWA

MathQuest is already well-positioned in several areas — it has a real teaching engine, a 3-step hint ladder, growth mindset language, CRA-aligned manipulatives (SVGs), a parent dashboard, and a 60-lesson scope across grades 3–6 that follows CCSS structure. The gaps below represent the delta between the current feature set and the credibility bar described in Section 2.

**Legend:** QW = Quick Win (days, no backend needed) | M = Medium (days to weeks, some design work) | L = Large (weeks, significant architecture or data)

---

### Gap 1: Explicit Common Core Standards Tagging

**What's missing**: Every skill/lesson in `js/curriculum/grade*.js` should carry a CCSS code (e.g., `"standard": "3.OA.A.7"`) that is visible in the UI and in the parent dashboard.

**Why it matters**: Teachers and homeschool parents consult CCSS codes when deciding what to practice. An app without visible standards tagging looks like an activity app, not a curriculum tool. IXL tags every one of its 8,500+ skills; Zearn publishes a scope-and-sequence PDF tied to CCSS codes.

**For an offline PWA**: This is entirely local data — add `standard` fields to curriculum JSON objects, surface the code in the lesson header and dashboard. No backend required.

**Effort**: QW — Add CCSS code strings to curriculum data objects and render them in the lesson view and dashboard. Two days of data entry plus one day of UI.

---

### Gap 2: Downloadable/Printable Scope & Sequence

**What's missing**: A human-readable, printable document that maps all 60 lessons to their CCSS code, grade level, and prerequisite structure. This is the document that justifies the curriculum to a parent or teacher.

**Why it matters**: SplashLearn, Zearn, IXL, and Khan Academy all publish scope and sequence documents publicly. Homeschool parents, especially, make curriculum choices based on these documents. The existing `docs/research/pedagogy.md` is close but is developer-facing, not parent-facing.

**For an offline PWA**: A static HTML page or a printable-formatted section of the parent dashboard. The app can auto-generate a scope-and-sequence view from the curriculum data files.

**Effort**: QW — Generate a "Curriculum Map" view in the parent/teacher area from existing curriculum data. One to two days.

---

### Gap 3: Diagnostic Placement Assessment

**What's missing**: MathQuest starts at Grade 3 content by default. There is no diagnostic that determines whether the child should start at Grade 3, Grade 4, or Grade 5, and there is no mechanism for identifying specific skill gaps within a grade.

**Why it matters**: IXL's diagnostic is one of its most-cited features by educators. DreamBox's core value proposition is that it meets each child where they are. Without placement, the app risks being either too easy (boring) or too hard (frustrating) for any given child.

**For an offline PWA**: A 20–30 item adaptive placement quiz at onboarding, covering representative skills from each grade. Results stored in localStorage determine the starting grade and flag any below-grade-level gaps. No backend or server-side adaptive engine needed — a branching decision tree based on correctness is sufficient for placement.

**Effort**: M — Requires designing the placement question set (roughly 5–8 questions per grade band, 20–30 total), writing a simple branching placement algorithm, and adding a placement onboarding flow. One to two weeks.

---

### Gap 4: Spaced Review of Mastered Skills

**What's missing**: When a child masters a skill (achieves the mastery threshold), that skill is never automatically revisited. Mastery decays without spaced retrieval practice; research consistently shows that skills not reviewed within 1–4 weeks are partially forgotten.

**Why it matters**: This is one of the most evidence-backed gaps. Khan Academy's Mastery Challenge, IXL's SmartScore decay model, and DreamBox's adaptive path all build spaced review into their core loop. Without it, MathQuest's mastery system gives a false sense of retention.

**For an offline PWA**: Entirely feasible in localStorage. Store a `lastPracticed` timestamp and a `masteryLevel` (0–3) per skill. In the home map and session logic, flag skills that are due for review (e.g., level 1 after 3 days, level 2 after 10 days, level 3 after 21 days — a simple Leitner-style schedule). Surface a "Review Time" prompt on the home screen.

**Effort**: M — The data model change is small (add `lastPracticed`, `reviewDue` fields). The scheduling logic is straightforward. The UI change (surfacing review prompts) is the main work. One to two weeks.

---

### Gap 5: Mixed/Interleaved Review Mode

**What's missing**: Practice sessions in MathQuest are topic-locked — the child practices one skill at a time. There is no mixed-review mode that draws problems from multiple previously learned skills in the same session.

**Why it matters**: Blocked practice (one skill at a time) produces faster short-term performance gains but weaker long-term retention than interleaved practice (mixed). This is one of the most replicated findings in learning science (Rohrer, Dunlosky, Kornell, etc.). IXL's "Recommendations" feature and Khan Academy's "Get a little practice" quizzes implement this. The absence of mixed review is a visible gap to any educator evaluating the app.

**For an offline PWA**: Can be implemented entirely locally. A "Mixed Review" mode selects problems from mastered or near-mastered skills across the current grade level. Logic: pull 2–3 problems each from the 5 most-recently-mastered skills and shuffle them.

**Effort**: M — Requires a new session mode, a problem-sampling function that queries across skills, and a UI entry point ("Mixed Review" button on home screen). Two weeks.

---

### Gap 6: Privacy Policy & COPPA Statement

**What's missing**: There is no privacy policy. The README states "no accounts, no internet, no API keys" — which is actually excellent for COPPA compliance — but this is not surfaced as a formal privacy statement accessible within the app.

**Why it matters**: Under the FTC's updated COPPA rule (April 2026 compliance deadline), any app directed at children must have a clear, accessible privacy policy regardless of whether it collects data. A parent or teacher evaluating MathQuest for home or classroom use will look for this. Its absence is an immediate credibility gap. The irony is that a fully offline, no-account app has the strongest possible privacy story — it just needs to be stated.

**For an offline PWA**: A static privacy page linked from the app footer and parent dashboard. The statement can honestly say: "MathQuest collects no personal data. All progress is stored exclusively on this device in your browser's local storage. No data is transmitted to any server. No accounts, no tracking, no ads." This is a stronger privacy statement than most competitors can make.

**Effort**: QW — Write a static HTML privacy page and link it from the app. One day.

---

### Gap 7: Accessibility Audit and Formal Statement

**What's missing**: The existing research docs mention WCAG 2.2 AA as a design goal and include good guidance on contrast, dyslexia fonts, and reduced motion. However, there has been no formal audit and no accessibility statement visible in the app.

**Why it matters**: ADA Title II (compliance deadline April 2027) is bringing accessibility requirements into school procurement decisions. Common Sense Education's rubric explicitly checks for accessibility features. The existing app likely meets many requirements already (dyslexia font toggle, reduced-motion mode, sound toggle) but needs a formal audit and a short statement of what is supported.

**For an offline PWA**: An offline app actually has an advantage here — no server-side rendering issues, no third-party scripts that break accessibility. Run a WAVE or Lighthouse accessibility audit. Fix the flagged issues (likely: focus indicators, ARIA labels for SVG manipulatives, alt text for mascot states). Add a one-paragraph accessibility statement to the parent dashboard.

**Effort**: M — Audit takes a few hours; remediation depends on findings but is typically one to two weeks for a focused codebase like this one.

---

### Gap 8: Mastery Check Transparency (Standards-Based Reporting)

**What's missing**: The parent dashboard shows accuracy and mastery-by-topic, but it is not tied to CCSS codes or grade-level benchmarks. A parent looking at the dashboard cannot tell whether their child is "at grade level" in any meaningful sense.

**Why it matters**: IXL's diagnostic gives parents a grade-level score by domain (Number Sense: Grade 4.3, Fractions: Grade 3.8). Zearn's teacher reports show skill mastery mapped to the CCSS scope and sequence. Parents increasingly expect this kind of standards-referenced reporting rather than raw accuracy percentages.

**For an offline PWA**: Once CCSS codes are added to curriculum data (Gap 1), the dashboard can display mastery organized by standard domain (Operations & Algebraic Thinking, Number & Operations — Fractions, etc.) with a clear indication of which grade-level skills are mastered, in progress, or not started. No backend needed.

**Effort**: M — Depends on Gap 1 being completed first. Dashboard UI redesign to organize by domain and standard. Two to three weeks.

---

### Gap 9: Printable Practice Worksheets

**What's missing**: No printable content. All practice is screen-based.

**Why it matters**: Teachers and homeschool parents use printable worksheets to supplement digital practice, reduce screen time, and send work home. SplashLearn, Prodigy, and Khan Academy all offer printables. The absence of printable content is a minor but real gap for school-use credibility.

**For an offline PWA**: The problem generator already creates unlimited fresh problems. A "Print Practice Sheet" function could use the existing generator to produce a browser-printable page of 10–15 problems (with or without answer key) for any skill. `window.print()` with a print-specific CSS is sufficient — no server, no PDF library required.

**Effort**: QW to M — Core functionality (print 10 problems for a skill) is one or two days. A polished, well-formatted worksheet is one additional day of CSS.

---

### Gap 10: Trust & Award Signals

**What's missing**: No credibility signals are visible within the app or on a product page. No Common Sense Education review, no award badges, no "as used by X families" counter, no research citations.

**Why it matters**: Parents and teachers make app adoption decisions partly on social proof and third-party validation. All leading products prominently display their awards (Apple Editor's Choice, Common Sense Education rating, ESSA evidence tier) on their landing pages.

**For an offline PWA / current project stage**: This is a longer-term signal that requires submitting the app to review organizations. Common Sense Education accepts submissions for review. The first step is having a public URL and a privacy policy (Gap 6). The IES What Works Clearinghouse and Evidence for ESSA require a published peer-reviewed study, making them multi-year horizons.

**Effort**: L — Getting reviewed by Common Sense Education requires a public deployment and a complete privacy policy. The review process itself is external. Realistic timeline: 3–6 months after public launch with privacy statement in place.

## 5. What MathQuest Already Does Well (Relative to the Market)

It is worth being explicit about what MathQuest has that many competitors do not:

- **True offline operation** with service worker caching is genuinely rare. DreamBox, IXL, Prodigy, and Zearn all require a network connection. MathQuest works on a plane, in a rural area, or on a device with no data plan. This is a real differentiator, not a limitation.
- **CRA-aligned SVG manipulatives** (number lines, base-ten blocks, arrays, fraction bars) built into the lesson engine — most flashcard-style apps skip this entirely.
- **3-step hint ladder + worked solutions** are pedagogically sophisticated. Many competitors (SplashLearn, Prodigy) show the answer after a wrong response without explanation.
- **Growth mindset language** throughout the UX is evidence-aligned. IXL's SmartScore decay mechanic (wrong answers subtract more than right answers add, causing visible score drops) is actually criticized by teachers for the opposite reason.
- **Zero-account, zero-tracking privacy story** is stronger than anything any online competitor can claim. Once formalized as a policy statement, this becomes a compelling differentiator for privacy-conscious families.
- **60-lesson scope across four grades** with word problems, multiple representations, and the CUBES strategy constitutes a genuine curriculum, not just drills.

The gap is primarily one of presentation and documentation — the pedagogical substance is largely there. Surfacing it with standards codes, a privacy statement, and a scope-and-sequence document converts MathQuest from "a well-made app" to "a credible curriculum tool."
