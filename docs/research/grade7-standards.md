# Grade 7 Common Core Mathematics Standards
## Implementation-Ready Reference

## Overview

Grade 7 is the gateway to formal algebra. The Common Core organizes 7th-grade math into five domains: **7.RP** (Ratios and Proportional Relationships), **7.NS** (The Number System), **7.EE** (Expressions and Equations), **7.G** (Geometry), and **7.SP** (Statistics and Probability). The major work of the grade — roughly 65–70% of instructional time — centers on proportional reasoning and extending arithmetic to all rational numbers, including negatives. Everything else in the grade is either supporting or additional work that reinforces the major clusters.

## Domain 1: 7.RP — Ratios and Proportional Relationships
*Cluster: Analyze proportional relationships and use them to solve real-world and mathematical problems.*

### The Big Idea (Kid-Friendly)
"When two quantities always change together in the same ratio — like $2 per song downloaded — that's a proportional relationship. You can spot it in a table, on a graph (straight line through the origin), or in an equation."

### Standards

**7.RP.A.1 — Unit Rates with Fractions**
Compute unit rates associated with ratios of fractions, including ratios of lengths, areas, and other quantities measured in like or different units. Example: if a person walks ½ mile in ¼ hour, the unit rate is (½)/(¼) = 2 miles per hour.
*Kid version: "How much of one thing for exactly ONE of the other?"*

**7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and Represent Proportional Relationships**
Decide whether two quantities are in a proportional relationship and represent it in multiple forms.
- **7.RP.A.2a** — Test for equivalent ratios in a table or check if a graph is a straight line through the origin.
- **7.RP.A.2b** — Identify the constant of proportionality (unit rate) in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.
- **7.RP.A.2c** — Represent proportional relationships with equations of the form y = kx.
- **7.RP.A.2d** — Explain what a point (x, y) on the graph means, especially (0, 0) and (1, r) where r is the unit rate.

**7.RP.A.3 — Percent Problems**
Use proportional relationships to solve multi-step ratio and percent problems, including: tax, tip, discounts, percent increase/decrease, simple interest, and commission.
*Kid version: "Percentages are just special ratios — parts out of 100."*

### Grade-6 Prerequisites
- **6.RP.A.1–3**: Understanding ratios, unit rates, and solving percent problems with simple visual models. Students can find 20% of a number but have not yet worked with percent increase/decrease or compound multi-step problems.
- **6.NS.B**: Familiarity with the number line and ordering of rational numbers (non-negative only at that point).

### Common Misconceptions — 7.RP
1. **Percents can't exceed 100.** Students see percent as "part of a whole" and struggle with 150% or a 200% increase. Correct by grounding in the ratio definition: 150% simply means a ratio of 150:100 = 1.5.
2. **Additive thinking for multiplicative relationships.** When a price increases from $20 to $30, students say "it went up by $10" rather than "it went up by 50%." This is the central conceptual hurdle of 7th-grade proportional reasoning.
3. **Setting up proportions upside-down.** Students write miles/hours on one side but hours/miles on the other, producing the reciprocal. Emphasize consistent unit placement.
4. **Confusing the constant of proportionality with the y-intercept.** In y = kx the line passes through (0, 0); students sometimes write k + something as the rule after exposure to non-proportional linear functions.
5. **Unit rate with fractional rates.** Computing (½)/(¼) as ½ × ¼ (multiplying instead of dividing). Reinforce complex fraction = division.

## Domain 2: 7.NS — The Number System
*Cluster: Apply and extend previous understandings of operations with fractions to add, subtract, multiply, and divide rational numbers.*

### The Big Idea (Kid-Friendly)
"Rational numbers include positives, negatives, fractions, and decimals. The rules you learned for whole numbers still work — we just extend them to cover numbers below zero."

### Standards

**7.NS.A.1 — Add and Subtract Rational Numbers**
Apply and extend previous understandings of addition and subtraction using a horizontal or vertical number line.
- **7.NS.A.1a** — Describe situations where opposite quantities combine to make 0 (additive inverses). Example: 5 + (−5) = 0, like a bank deposit canceling a debt.
- **7.NS.A.1b** — Understand p + q as the number located a distance |q| from p, in the direction determined by the sign of q.
- **7.NS.A.1c** — Subtraction is adding the additive inverse: p − q = p + (−q).
- **7.NS.A.1d** — Apply properties of operations (commutative, associative, distributive) to add and subtract rational numbers.

**7.NS.A.2 — Multiply and Divide Rational Numbers**
- **7.NS.A.2a** — Understand sign rules for multiplication: negative × negative = positive, justified by requiring the distributive property to hold.
- **7.NS.A.2b** — Every quotient of two integers (non-zero divisor) is rational. Division by zero is undefined.
- **7.NS.A.2c** — Apply properties of operations to multiply and divide rational numbers.
- **7.NS.A.2d** — Convert a rational number to a decimal using long division; know the result always terminates or repeats.

**7.NS.A.3 — All Four Operations with Rational Numbers**
Solve real-world and mathematical problems using addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division of rational numbers (whole numbers, fractions, and decimals combined in the same problem).
*Kid version: "Mix and match any kind of number — positive, negative, fraction, decimal — with any operation."*

### Grade-6 Prerequisites
- **6.NS.C.5–7**: Understanding positive and negative numbers as describing opposite quantities; plotting on a number line; absolute value; comparing integers.
- **6.NS.A.1**: Dividing fractions by fractions (the most demanding prerequisite — students who skipped 6.NS.A.1 will struggle with complex fraction unit rates in 7.RP.A.1).
- **6.EE.A.3**: Applying properties of operations to expressions with positive rational numbers only.

### Common Misconceptions — 7.NS
1. **Subtracting negatives: "two minuses make a plus."** Students apply this rule as rote without understanding. They then misapply it in contexts like −3 − (−5) = −8 (adding both absolute values). The number-line model is the anchor: subtracting a negative means moving in the positive direction.
2. **Sign of the sum vs. difference.** Students compute −8 + 5 as −13 (adding absolute values regardless of sign) or as +3 (ignoring the larger absolute value). Reinforce: same sign → add and keep sign; different signs → subtract and keep the sign of the larger absolute value.
3. **Negative × negative is negative (intuition error).** Most students expect negativity to "spread." Motivate the positive product through the pattern: 3×(−2)=−6, 2×(−2)=−4, 1×(−2)=−2, 0×(−2)=0, −1×(−2)=+2.
4. **Fraction arithmetic with negatives.** Students correctly reduce ¾ − ¼ but get confused on −¾ − (−¼). Emphasize rewriting as addition first.
5. **All repeating decimals are irrational.** Students conflate "never ending" with irrational. 1/3 = 0.333... is perfectly rational.

## Domain 3: 7.EE — Expressions and Equations
*Clusters: (A) Use properties of operations to generate equivalent expressions. (B) Solve real-life and mathematical problems using numerical and algebraic expressions and equations.*

### The Big Idea (Kid-Friendly)
"Variables are placeholders for unknown numbers. You can rearrange expressions like a mathematical puzzle, then write and solve equations to find the unknown."

### Standards

**7.EE.A.1 — Equivalent Expressions with Rational Coefficients**
Apply properties of operations (distributive, commutative, associative) to add, subtract, factor, and expand linear expressions with rational coefficients.
Example: 1.2x + 3 − 0.4x = 0.8x + 3; or 3(x + ¼) = 3x + ¾.
*Kid version: "Combine like terms and use the distributive property — even when the numbers are fractions or decimals."*

**7.EE.A.2 — Rewriting Expressions to Reveal Meaning**
Understand that rewriting an expression in a different form can clarify the problem context. Example: a + 0.07a = 1.07a shows that adding 7% tax is the same as multiplying the price by 1.07.
*This is the conceptual bridge from arithmetic to algebraic thinking.*

**7.EE.B.3 — Multi-Step Problems with Rational Numbers**
Solve multi-step real-life problems with positive and negative rational numbers in any form, using tools strategically. Assess reasonableness using mental computation and estimation. Convert between forms (fraction ↔ decimal ↔ percent) as needed.

**7.EE.B.4 — Write and Solve Equations and Inequalities**
Use variables to represent unknown quantities and construct simple equations or inequalities.
- **7.EE.B.4a** — Solve word problems leading to equations of the form px + q = r and p(x + q) = r, where p, q, r are specific rational numbers. Example: "A gym charges $30 a month plus $5 per class. You spent $65. How many classes did you attend? 30 + 5x = 65."
- **7.EE.B.4b** — Solve word problems leading to inequalities px + q > r or px + q < r, graph the solution on a number line, and interpret in context. Example: "You need to earn more than $50: 8h + 6 > 50."

### Grade-6 Prerequisites
- **6.EE.A.1–4**: Writing and evaluating expressions, equivalent expressions, identifying parts of an expression.
- **6.EE.B.5–7**: Solving one-step equations using substitution and properties of equality.
- **6.EE.B.8**: Writing inequalities and representing solutions on a number line (one-step only at grade 6).

### Common Misconceptions — 7.EE
1. **Combining unlike terms.** Students write 5x + 4 = 9x because the addition habit overrides the "like terms" rule. Concrete analogy: 5 apples + 4 oranges ≠ 9 apples.
2. **Confusing simplifying with solving.** When given 4x + 2x = 18, some students combine like terms to get 6x and stop. Others attempt to divide both sides by x first. Clarify: simplify the expression side, then isolate the variable.
3. **Distributing only to the first term.** Students write 3(x + 4) = 3x + 4, forgetting to multiply 3 × 4. Distribute to every term inside.
4. **Direction of inequality flips.** Students rarely apply the sign-flip rule when multiplying/dividing both sides by a negative. Use number-line examples: 2 < 4, so −2 > −4.
5. **px + q = r vs. p(x + q) = r confusion.** The two structures have different solution paths; students substitute the wrong approach. Emphasize reading the problem structure first.

## Domain 4: 7.G — Geometry
*Clusters: (A) Draw, construct, and describe geometrical figures and relationships. (B) Solve real-life problems involving angle measure, area, surface area, and volume.*

### The Big Idea (Kid-Friendly)
"Geometry at grade 7 is about scale (zooming shapes in and out), angle relationships, and calculating measurements for flat and 3D shapes — including the special case of circles."

### Standards

**7.G.A.1 — Scale Drawings**
Solve problems involving scale drawings of geometric figures, including computing actual lengths and areas from a scale drawing, and reproducing a scale drawing at a different scale.
*Kid version: "If the map says 1 inch = 10 miles, every measurement on the map is a proportional relationship."*

**7.G.A.2 — Constructing Triangles**
Draw geometric shapes with given conditions (freehand, ruler-and-protractor, or technology). Focus on triangles: when do three given side lengths or three given angles determine a unique triangle, more than one, or no triangle at all?
*Kid version: "Not any three lengths make a triangle — the two shorter sides must add up to more than the longest."*

**7.G.A.3 — Cross Sections of 3D Figures**
Describe the two-dimensional shapes that result from slicing three-dimensional figures (plane sections of right rectangular prisms and right rectangular pyramids). Slicing horizontally, vertically, or diagonally produces different 2D shapes.

**7.G.B.4 — Circles: Area and Circumference**
Know the formulas C = πd = 2πr and A = πr², use them to solve problems, and give an informal derivation of the relationship between circumference and area.
*Kid version: "Pi is the ratio of any circle's circumference to its diameter — always the same mysterious number (~3.14)."*

**7.G.B.5 — Angle Relationships**
Use facts about supplementary (add to 180°), complementary (add to 90°), vertical (equal when two lines cross), and adjacent angles to write and solve simple equations for unknown angles.
*Kid version: "Angles have relationships with each other — use them like clues to find the missing one."*

**7.G.B.6 — Area, Surface Area, and Volume of Composite Figures**
Solve real-world problems involving area and surface area of two- and three-dimensional objects made of triangles, quadrilaterals, polygons, cubes, and right prisms.
*Kid version: "Surface area = wrapping paper needed; volume = how much fits inside."*

### Grade-6 Prerequisites
- **6.G.A.1**: Area of triangles, quadrilaterals, and composite shapes on the coordinate plane.
- **6.G.A.2**: Volume of right rectangular prisms with fractional edge lengths (V = Bh).
- **6.G.A.4**: Surface area of 3D figures using nets.
- **6.RP.A**: Ratio reasoning for scale (foundational for 7.G.A.1).

### Common Misconceptions — 7.G
1. **Confusing circumference and area formulas.** Students mix up C = 2πr and A = πr², often using the diameter where the radius is required. Mnemonic: area "squares the r" because it covers a 2D region.
2. **Scale factors and area.** If a scale factor is 3, students think the area also scales by 3 instead of 9. Area scales by the square of the linear scale factor.
3. **Surface area vs. volume.** Students apply volume formulas when asked for surface area (and vice versa). Surface area is 2D (square units) and covers the outside; volume is 3D (cubic units) and fills the inside.
4. **Triangle inequality intuition.** Students assume any three lengths form a triangle. Test: 3, 4, 8 do not (3 + 4 < 8).
5. **Vertical angles vs. supplementary angles.** Students conflate the two: vertical angles are equal; supplementary angles add to 180°. A common error is assuming that all angle pairs formed by intersecting lines are supplementary.
6. **Cross sections and mental rotation.** Students struggle to visualize what slice a plane makes through a pyramid. Physical or digital models help significantly.

## Domain 5: 7.SP — Statistics and Probability
*Clusters: (A) Use random sampling to draw inferences about a population. (B) Draw informal comparative inferences about two populations. (C) Investigate chance processes and develop, use, and evaluate probability models.*

### The Big Idea (Kid-Friendly)
"Statistics lets you make smart guesses about a whole group by studying a sample. Probability tells you how likely events are — from impossible (0) to certain (1)."

### Standards

**7.SP.A.1 — Random Sampling and Valid Inferences**
Understand that statistics can give information about a population by examining a sample; valid inferences require a representative sample. Random sampling tends to produce representative samples.

**7.SP.A.2 — Using Samples to Draw Inferences**
Use data from a random sample to draw inferences about a population with an unknown characteristic. Generate multiple simulated samples of the same size to gauge variation in estimates.

**7.SP.B.3 — Visual Overlap of Two Distributions**
Informally assess the degree of visual overlap of two numerical data distributions with similar variability, measuring the difference between centers as a multiple of the measure of variability (an informal effect size).

**7.SP.B.4 — Comparative Inferences About Two Populations**
Use measures of center (mean, median) and measures of variability (mean absolute deviation, interquartile range) from random samples to draw informal comparative inferences.
*Kid version: "Compare two groups using their averages AND their spread — a small average difference might not matter if the data is very spread out."*

**7.SP.C.5 — Probability as a Number Between 0 and 1**
Understand that probability is a number from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain); ~0.5 means equally likely or unlikely.

**7.SP.C.6 — Experimental Probability**
Approximate the probability of an event by collecting data on the process (long-run relative frequency). Predict the approximate relative frequency given a probability.

**7.SP.C.7 — Probability Models**
Develop a probability model and use it to find probabilities of events; compare model predictions to observed frequencies.
- **7.SP.C.7a** — Uniform probability models (all outcomes equally likely).
- **7.SP.C.7b** — Non-uniform probability models (e.g., a weighted spinner).

**7.SP.C.8 — Compound Events**
Find probabilities of compound events using organized lists, tables, tree diagrams, and simulation.
- **7.SP.C.8a** — Understand that the probability of a compound event equals the fraction of sample-space outcomes in which the event occurs.
- **7.SP.C.8b** — Represent sample spaces for compound events using lists, tables, and tree diagrams.
- **7.SP.C.8c** — Design and use simulations to generate frequencies for compound events.

### Grade-6 Prerequisites
- **6.SP.A.1–3**: Statistical questions, distribution shape, center, and spread; dot plots, histograms, box plots.
- **6.SP.B.4–5**: Summarizing numerical data in context; computing mean, median, mode, and range.
- **6.RP.A.3c**: Percent as a ratio (foundational for expressing probability as a fraction or percent).

### Common Misconceptions — 7.SP
1. **Gambler's fallacy.** Students believe past outcomes affect future independent events ("it's been heads 5 times, so tails is overdue"). Reiterate independence: each coin flip resets.
2. **Small samples are representative.** Students trust a sample of 5 to represent a population of 1,000. Counter with simulation: generate multiple samples and show the variation.
3. **P(event) must be a "nice" fraction.** Students expect exact answers like ½ or ¼ and are confused by experimental probabilities like 7/20. Emphasize that experimental probability is an approximation.
4. **Confusing theoretical and experimental probability.** Students expect flipping a coin 10 times to produce exactly 5 heads. Use the law of large numbers — the approximation improves with more trials.
5. **Sample space enumeration errors for compound events.** Students omit outcomes when listing combinations, especially when order matters. Tree diagrams and organized tables are essential scaffolds.
6. **Mean vs. median choice.** Students default to mean when median is more appropriate (e.g., when data is skewed by an outlier). Grade 7 introduces this judgment informally.

## Major Work of Grade 7

Per Achieve the Core's Focus document, the **major clusters** that deserve the majority of instructional time are:

- **7.RP.A** — Analyze proportional relationships (major)
- **7.NS.A** — Apply and extend operations to rational numbers (major)
- **7.EE.A–B** — Expressions, equations, and inequalities (major)

The **supporting clusters** that reinforce and connect to major work:
- **7.G.A** — Scale drawings and geometric figures (supporting)
- **7.SP.A–B** — Sampling and inference (supporting)

The **additional clusters** (important but receive less emphasis):
- **7.G.B** — Angle, area, surface area, volume (additional)
- **7.SP.C** — Probability (additional)

A critical implication: do not spend equal time on all five domains. 7.RP + 7.NS + 7.EE together represent the core of pre-algebra and should occupy roughly two-thirds of practice time.

## Recommended ~14-Skill Mastery Sequence

The order below is designed so each skill builds on the one before it, matches the coherence structure of the CCSS, and reflects how leading open-curriculum programs (e.g., Illustrative Math / Open Up Resources) sequence the year.

| # | Skill Title | Standard(s) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Unit Rates and Simplifying Ratios | 7.RP.A.1 | Extends ratio work from grade 6; required before proportional equations |
| 2 | Proportional Relationships: Tables and Graphs | 7.RP.A.2a, 2b, 2d | Core representation work before algebraic form |
| 3 | Equations for Proportional Relationships | 7.RP.A.2c | Bridges ratios to algebra (y = kx) |
| 4 | Percent Problems: Discount, Tax, Tip, Percent Change | 7.RP.A.3 | Application of proportional reasoning; unlocks real-world context |
| 5 | Scale Drawings | 7.G.A.1 | Immediately applies proportional reasoning geometrically |
| 6 | Adding and Subtracting Rational Numbers (Integers Focus) | 7.NS.A.1a–d | Fundamental number sense for all subsequent algebra |
| 7 | Multiplying and Dividing Rational Numbers | 7.NS.A.2a–d | Completes rational number arithmetic; enables expressions |
| 8 | All Four Operations with Rational Numbers | 7.NS.A.3 | Mixed-operation fluency required for equations |
| 9 | Simplifying Linear Expressions | 7.EE.A.1 | First algebraic manipulation; requires 7.NS fluency |
| 10 | Solving Two-Step Equations (px + q = r) | 7.EE.B.4a | Core algebra skill; direct preparation for 8th-grade systems |
| 11 | Solving and Graphing Inequalities | 7.EE.B.4b | Natural extension of equation solving; adds sign-flip rule |
| 12 | Circles: Circumference and Area | 7.G.B.4 | Applies proportional reasoning (π = C/d) and rational arithmetic |
| 13 | Area, Surface Area, and Volume of Composite Figures | 7.G.B.6, 7.G.B.5 | Applies expression writing and two-step equations in geometry |
| 14 | Probability: Models and Compound Events | 7.SP.C.5–8 | Consolidates fraction arithmetic; engaging capstone unit |

*Note: 7.G.A.2 (triangle construction), 7.G.A.3 (cross sections), 7.SP.A–B (sampling/inference) are woven into the sequence as shorter supporting lessons rather than standalone full skills, keeping the 14-skill track tight.*
