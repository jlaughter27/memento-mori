# Math Tutor Curriculum Research
## Grades 3–6 Scope, Explanations, Misconceptions & Tutor Language

**Audience:** Children ages 7–11, primary user is an 8-year-old (Grade 3, homeschool)  
**Purpose:** Reference for the app's explanation engine — exact phrasings, manipulatives, and misconception fixes

---

## Part 1: Scope & Sequence by Grade (Common Core)

### Grade 3 — The Multiplication & Division Year
**Major focus:** Multiplication and division of whole numbers; fractions as numbers on a number line.

| Domain | Key Skills |
|---|---|
| Operations & Algebraic Thinking | Understand multiplication as equal groups and repeated addition; understand division as sharing or grouping; know all multiplication facts 0–10 by year end; solve one- and two-step word problems |
| Number & Operations — Fractions | Understand unit fractions (1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1/6, 1/8); place fractions on a number line; recognize equivalent fractions using visual models |
| Measurement & Data | Tell and write time; measure and estimate liquid volumes; understand area as tiling with unit squares |
| Geometry | Understand categories of shapes (quadrilaterals, etc.) |

**Grade 3 priority skill:** Multiplication fact fluency (0–100). Everything else in grades 4–6 depends on it.

---

### Grade 4 — Multi-Digit Operations & Fraction Equivalence
**Major focus:** Multi-digit multiplication and division; fraction equivalence and ordering; decimal introduction.

| Domain | Key Skills |
|---|---|
| Number & Operations — Base Ten | Multiply up to 4-digit × 1-digit; multiply 2-digit × 2-digit; divide with remainders; understand place value to one million |
| Number & Operations — Fractions | Equivalent fractions; compare fractions with different denominators; add/subtract fractions with like denominators; multiply a fraction by a whole number; introduction to decimals (tenths/hundredths) |
| Measurement | Convert units within a system (feet to inches, kg to g) |
| Geometry | Lines, angles, symmetry |

**Grade 4 priority skill:** Area model and partial products for multi-digit multiplication — the bridge to the standard algorithm.

---

### Grade 5 — Fractions All Operations & Decimals
**Major focus:** Full fraction operations; decimal operations; volume.

| Domain | Key Skills |
|---|---|
| Number & Operations — Fractions | Add and subtract fractions with unlike denominators; multiply fractions by fractions; divide unit fractions by whole numbers and whole numbers by unit fractions |
| Number & Operations — Base Ten | Multi-digit decimal operations; understand place value to thousandths; standard algorithm for multiplication |
| Measurement & Data | Volume of rectangular prisms |
| Operations & Algebraic Thinking | Write and interpret numerical expressions; introduce order of operations (no exponents yet) |

**Grade 5 priority skill:** Unlike-denominator fraction addition — finding common denominators is the central challenge.

---

### Grade 6 — Ratios, Integers & Introduction to Algebra
**Major focus:** Ratios and proportional reasoning; rational numbers (including negatives); early algebra.

| Domain | Key Skills |
|---|---|
| Ratios & Proportional Relationships | Understand ratios; unit rates; percent as rate per 100; ratio tables and double number lines |
| The Number System | Divide fractions by fractions; understand positive and negative numbers on a number line; absolute value |
| Expressions & Equations | Write and evaluate algebraic expressions; solve one-step equations and inequalities |
| Statistics & Probability | Mean, median, mode, range; histograms; box plots |

**Grade 6 priority skill:** Ratio reasoning — the foundation of proportional thinking that carries through middle school.

## Part 2: How to Explain Key Concepts

### Topic A: Multiplication

#### Progression (always follow this order)

**Step 1 — Equal Groups (Grade 3 entry)**  
Multiplication is "groups of." Before any symbols, build it physically.

- **Manipulatives:** Counters, small toys, egg cartons, muffin tins
- **Language:** *"3 × 4 means 3 groups with 4 in each group. Let's build it — three piles, put 4 beans in each pile. Now count them all up."*
- **Connect to addition:** *"See how we added 4 + 4 + 4? Multiplication is a faster way to say that."*

**Step 2 — Arrays (Grade 3–4)**  
An array shows multiplication as rows and columns.

- **Manipulatives:** Grid paper, square tiles, graph paper dots
- **Language:** *"An array for 3 × 4 has 3 rows and 4 columns. Each row has the same number — that's what makes it multiplication. And here's the cool part: turn the paper sideways. Now it's 4 rows of 3. Same answer! That's why 3 × 4 and 4 × 3 are always equal."*
- **Key insight to name:** The commutative property — it cuts the facts table in half.

**Step 3 — Area Model / Box Method (Grade 4–5)**  
Draw a rectangle, split it into place-value parts.

- **Manipulatives:** Graph paper, dry-erase grid mats, base-ten block arrays
- **Worked example for 23 × 14:**
  ```
  Break apart: 23 = 20 + 3    14 = 10 + 4
  Draw a box with 2 rows × 2 columns:
      | 20  |  3  |
  10  | 200 |  30 |
   4  |  80 |  12 |
  Add the parts: 200 + 30 + 80 + 12 = 322
  ```
- **Language:** *"We're going to break the big numbers into friendlier pieces, multiply the pieces, then add them back. Each box is its own little multiplication — you already know all of those!"*

**Step 4 — Standard Algorithm (Grade 5)**  
Only introduce after area model is solid. The algorithm is a compressed version of the area model, not a different thing.

- **Language:** *"Remember our boxes? The standard algorithm does the exact same math — it just writes it in a shorter way. Let me show you how each step matches a box we already filled."*

### Topic B: Long Division

Long division is introduced in Grade 4–5. It should come after students understand division conceptually (sharing/grouping) and partial quotients.

#### Prerequisites to check
- Solid multiplication fact fluency (0–9)
- Understands division as "how many groups of ___ fit in ___?"

#### Partial Quotients First (Grade 4)
Before the standard algorithm, use partial quotients — it's more transparent.

- **Worked example: 156 ÷ 4**
  - *"How many 4s fit in 156? Let's make easy guesses. I know 4 × 10 = 40. So 10 groups of 4 fit. Write 10 to the side, subtract 40. Left over: 116. Can I take another 10 groups? Yes — subtract 40 again. Left: 76. Another 10? Yes — subtract 40. Left: 36. Now 4 × 9 = 36 — take 9 groups. Left: 0. Add up guesses: 10 + 10 + 10 + 9 = 39."*

#### Standard Long Division Algorithm (Grade 4–5)
Use the mnemonic **Does McDonald's Serve Cheese Burgers?**  
→ **D**ivide, **M**ultiply, **S**ubtract, **C**heck (bring down), **B**ring down, **R**epeat.

- **Manipulatives:** Graph paper to keep columns aligned; place-value chart on the side
- **Language:** *"We're going to work through this step by step, like a recipe. Each time we finish one step, we always go back to the top of the recipe."*
- **Spatial tip:** Have the child write the steps vertically down the left margin: D, M, S, C, B. They check off each one.

### Topic C: Fractions

#### The central idea to establish first
*"A fraction tells you two things: how many equal pieces the whole was cut into (the denominator), AND how many of those pieces you have (the numerator)."*

The word "denominator" = denominator/denomination = what kind of piece. The numerator counts them.

#### Fraction Bars / Strips (Grade 3 entry)
- **Manipulatives:** Paper fraction strips, printed fraction bars, physical fraction tiles
- **Language:** *"Let's fold a paper strip into 4 equal parts. Each part is one-fourth — 1/4. Now fold another into 2 equal parts. Each part is one-half — 1/2. Which piece is bigger? You can see it right there."*
- **Key rule:** *"Always keep the whole strip drawn or visible. The fraction only makes sense compared to the whole."*

#### Number Lines for Fractions (Grade 3–4)
Number lines are critical — they show fractions as points, not just pieces of a shape.

- **Language:** *"Draw a line from 0 to 1. That whole line is our 'one whole.' Now split it into 4 equal jumps. Each jump lands you on 1/4, 2/4, 3/4, 4/4. See where 2/4 lands? That's the same spot as 1/2! That's what equivalent fractions means — different names, same location."*

#### Equivalent Fractions (Grade 3–4)
- **Manipulatives:** Fraction bars side by side, number line with two sets of tick marks
- **Language:** *"We're going to see if these two fractions are secret twins — they look different but they're the same amount. Let me show you with the bars… see? 1/2 and 2/4 are the same length! On the number line, they land on the exact same dot."*
- **The multiply/divide rule (Grade 4):** *"Whatever you do to the bottom, do the same to the top. Multiply top and bottom by 2: 1/2 becomes 2/4. You haven't changed the amount — only how you're describing it."*

#### Adding Fractions with Unlike Denominators (Grade 5)
- **Language:** *"Imagine you're adding apples and oranges — you can't just count them as one thing. We need to rename both fractions so they're talking about the same size pieces. Find a number that both denominators can fit into…"*
- **Worked example: 1/3 + 1/4**
  - *"3 and 4 both fit into 12. So 1/3 = 4/12, and 1/4 = 3/12. Now they're the same kind of piece — add the tops: 4 + 3 = 7. Answer: 7/12."*

### Topic D: Place Value and Base-Ten Blocks

#### The concrete-to-abstract ladder
Always go: **Physical blocks → Picture/drawing → Symbols**. Never skip straight to symbols.

- **Unit cube = 1**
- **Rod (10 cubes long) = 10**
- **Flat (10 rods wide) = 100**
- **Large cube = 1,000**

#### Regrouping in Addition
- **Language:** *"We have 14 ones. But our ones column can only hold 0–9. So we need to trade 10 of those ones for a tens rod and carry it over. We're not making up a new number — we're just writing it in a tidy way."*
- **Preferred word:** "Regroup" or "trade" — not "carry" (which hides the meaning).

#### Regrouping in Subtraction
- **Language:** *"We need to take away 7 ones, but we only have 3 ones. Can our tens column help? Yes — trade one ten-rod for 10 ones. Now we have 13 ones and one fewer ten. We haven't changed the total amount — just rearranged it."*
- **Preferred word:** "Trade" or "regroup" — not "borrow" (you never give it back).
- **Visual anchor:** *"Think of it like breaking a $10 bill into ten $1 bills. Still the same money, just different packaging."*

---

### Topic E: Decimals (Grade 4–5)

#### Connect immediately to fractions
The single most powerful move: read 0.5 as "five-tenths" not "zero point five" — the fraction lives in the name.

- **Language:** *"A decimal is just a fraction in disguise. The decimal point shows where the ones place ends and the parts-of-one begin. One place right of the point = tenths. Two places right = hundredths. So 0.37 is 37 hundredths — which is the same as 37/100."*

#### Manipulatives for decimals
- Base-ten blocks: *redefine the flat as "one whole"* — now the rod is one-tenth and the unit cube is one-hundredth
- Money: pennies (0.01), dimes (0.10), dollar bills (1.00)
- Number line: zoom in between 0 and 1 to show tenths; zoom in between 0.3 and 0.4 to show hundredths

#### Comparing decimals
- **Language:** *"Line up the decimal points, then compare place by place — just like comparing whole numbers, starting from the left. Don't count digits; look at the place value."*

### Topic F: Word Problem Strategies

#### CUBES (Grades 3–6)
A step-by-step marking routine:

| Letter | Action | Tutor phrasing |
|---|---|---|
| **C** — Circle | Circle all the numbers | *"What numbers do we see? Circle each one."* |
| **U** — Underline | Underline the question | *"What is this problem actually asking us to find? Underline it."* |
| **B** — Box | Box key math words | *"Any words that tell you what to DO? Box them — words like 'total,' 'left over,' 'each,' 'shared.'"* |
| **E** — Evaluate/Eliminate | Cross out info you don't need | *"Is there any information we won't use? Cross it out so it doesn't confuse us."* |
| **S** — Solve and check | Solve, then re-read the question | *"Does our answer actually answer the underlined question? Check the units — is it asking for dollars, kids, or cookies?"* |

#### Draw a Picture / Bar Model (highly recommended addition)
Before calculating, draw what the problem describes.

- **Language:** *"Before we write a single number, let's draw what's happening. If the problem says 'Maya has 3 bags with 8 apples each,' draw 3 boxes and write 8 in each one. Now we can see the multiplication."*
- Bar models (tape diagrams) are especially powerful for comparison and fraction problems in grades 4–6.

## Part 3: Common Misconceptions and Gentle Fixes

### General Correction Philosophy

Research consistently shows that framing mistakes as learning moments — not failures — improves both math outcomes and persistence. Recommended tutor language:

- *"That's a really interesting answer — let me show you what your brain was doing, because it makes sense in a way…"*
- *"This is exactly the kind of mistake that teaches us something important."*
- *"You almost had it — let's find where the path turned."*

Never say: "That's wrong" without immediately following with a concrete why and a try-again.

---

### Multiplication Misconceptions

| Common Mistake | Why It Happens | Gentle Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Confuses 3×4 with 3+4 | Both are addition-related; symbols look different but effects feel similar | Build 3 groups of 4 physically. *"3+4 is one group of 3 and one group of 4 combined. 3×4 is three groups of four — very different!"* |
| "Multiplication always makes bigger" | True for whole numbers — until fractions and decimals appear | *"What is 1/2 × 8? We're taking half of 8 — we get 4, which is smaller! Multiplying by something less than 1 makes things smaller."* Predict and check: *"Will 0.5 × 6 be bigger or smaller than 6?"* |
| Forgets to multiply all partial products in area model | Loses track of which boxes they've filled | Color each box a different color as it's filled; use a physical grid so nothing gets skipped |
| Skips regrouping in standard algorithm | Steps not internalized as place-value actions | Return to area model; match each step in the algorithm to a box: *"This 2 we 'carried' is actually the tens from this box."* |

---

### Division Misconceptions

| Common Mistake | Why It Happens | Gentle Fix |
|---|---|---|
| "Division always makes smaller" | True for whole-number divisors — breaks with decimals/fractions | *"What is 6 ÷ 1/2? How many half-cups fit in 6 cups? 12! Dividing by a fraction makes the answer bigger."* |
| Loses track of which step they're on in long division | Cognitive overload from multi-step algorithm | Use the mnemonic checklist written on paper; check off each step physically |
| Places the quotient digit in the wrong column | Column alignment issues | Graph paper with one digit per square; large visual place-value headers |
| Remainder confusion — writes it as leftover rather than understanding it | Doesn't connect remainder to real meaning | *"The remainder is what's left after we've made as many equal groups as we can. If we're sharing 17 cookies among 5 friends, 3 cookies don't get shared — that's the remainder."* |

---

### Fractions Misconceptions

| Common Mistake | Why It Happens | Gentle Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Larger denominator = larger fraction (e.g., thinks 1/8 > 1/4) | Whole-number logic applied: 8 > 4 | *"The denominator tells us how many pieces the whole was cut into. More pieces means each piece is SMALLER. If you cut a pizza into 8 slices, each slice is tinier than if you cut it into 4."* Show with fraction bars. |
| Adds denominators when adding fractions (1/2 + 1/2 = 2/4) | Treats numerator and denominator as two separate whole numbers | *"The denominator just tells us what kind of piece we have — it doesn't get added. 1 apple + 1 apple = 2 apples, not 2 pears."* |
| Thinks a fraction with no numerator shown equals the denominator (e.g., "the fraction is 4") | Whole-number thinking | Constantly return to the verbal form: *"Say it out loud — 'one-fourth.' Now write what you said."* |
| Equivalent fractions: doesn't know why multiplying top and bottom by the same number works | Procedure without understanding | Use fraction strips physically: *"Fold the strip again — you made more pieces, but the same amount of the whole is colored."* Then connect to the multiply rule. |
| Comparing fractions with different denominators by only looking at numerators | Overgeneralizes "bigger number = bigger fraction" | Always draw fraction bars or number lines before comparing. *"Which is bigger, 3/4 or 3/5? Draw both bars the same length, cut one into 4 pieces, one into 5. Shade 3 in each. Which colored part is longer?"* |

---

### Place Value & Regrouping Misconceptions

| Common Mistake | Why It Happens | Gentle Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Regroups when not necessary (subtracts every time regardless) | Follows a trick ("if the top is smaller, borrow") mechanically | *"Before we do anything, look at the ones column: do we NEED more ones? Only regroup if the top number is smaller than the bottom."* Have child check each column before starting. |
| After regrouping, forgets to reduce the tens digit | Multi-step procedure, working memory strain | Use base-ten blocks; physically remove the rod and replace with 10 units. The physical act burns in the adjustment. |
| Writes numbers based on digit count, not place (e.g., "one hundred twenty three" = 100203) | Doesn't understand zero as a placeholder | *"Let's build it with blocks first, then write what we built. We have 1 flat, 2 rods, 3 units. Write a digit for each place — ones, tens, hundreds. If there are no tens, write a 0 to hold that spot."* |

---

### Decimal Misconceptions

| Common Mistake | Why It Happens | Gentle Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Thinks 0.10 > 0.9 because "10 is bigger than 9" | Ignores place value, treats decimal digits as whole numbers | *"Line up the decimal points. Now compare: which has more tenths? 0.9 has 9 tenths, 0.10 has 1 tenth. 9 tenths wins!"* Use a number line to show both. |
| Adds decimals without aligning decimal points | Treats as whole numbers | *"The decimal point is your anchor. Always stack problems so the decimal points are in a perfect vertical line — then everything lines up correctly."* |
| Reads 0.3 and 0.30 as different amounts | Doesn't understand trailing zeros | *"0.3 is 3 tenths. 0.30 is 30 hundredths. Are those the same? Yes — use the fraction bars to check: 3/10 = 30/100."* |

---

### Word Problem Misconceptions

| Common Mistake | Why It Happens | Gentle Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Grabs all numbers and adds them | Doesn't comprehend the problem structure | *"Before you touch a single number, cover the numbers with your finger and read the problem. What is happening in this story?"* |
| Uses the operation that was practiced most recently ("last lesson bias") | Pattern-matching rather than comprehending | Draw a picture first, always. *"Draw me what's happening. Now what do you need to find?"* |
| Answers a different question than asked | Stops after first calculation, doesn't re-read question | CUBES step S: *"Re-read the underlined question. Did we answer THAT, or something else?"* |
| Ignores or misuses remainder in context | Division remainder not connected to real-world meaning | *"The remainder is real-world info. If the question asks 'how many buses are needed?' and there are 3 kids left over after filling buses, you need one more bus. The remainder changes the answer!"* |

## Part 5: Grade-by-Grade Tutor Phrase Bank

### Grade 3 (primary user — age 8)
- *"How many groups? How many in each group? Let's build it."*
- *"3 times 4 means 3 groups of 4. Say it back to me."*
- *"What do you notice about the array when I turn it sideways?"*
- *"This fraction is telling you how many pieces the whole thing was cut into, and how many you have."*
- *"Let's put that fraction on our number line. Where does it live between 0 and 1?"*

### Grade 4
- *"Let's use our area model box. What are the two numbers we're multiplying? Break them apart by place value."*
- *"Before we subtract, look at the ones column. Do we have enough, or do we need to trade a ten?"*
- *"These two fractions look different, but are they the same amount? How could you check?"*
- *"0.4 — say the full name: four-tenths. Now write it as a fraction."*

### Grade 5
- *"To add these fractions, I need pieces that are the same size. What's the smallest number both denominators fit into?"*
- *"Does multiplying this make it bigger or smaller? Let's predict before we calculate."*
- *"Show me the partial quotients method first — then we'll see how the long division shortcut matches."*

### Grade 6
- *"A ratio is a comparison. 3:5 means for every 3 of this, there are 5 of that."*
- *"What's the unit rate? Find out how much per ONE."*
- *"On this number line, negative numbers are to the left of zero. How far is -4 from 0?"*
